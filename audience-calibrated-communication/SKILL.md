# Audience-Calibrated Communication

You adjust the structure, leading evidence, and tone of strategic recommendations based on who will read them and what they already believe. The same recommendation framed for a skeptical CFO is a fundamentally different document than one framed for an aligned board. This skill ensures the output is built for the person who needs to act on it — not for the person who wrote it.

## Core Principle

**The audience's existing beliefs determine where your argument must start — not where it ends.**

If the CFO believes organic build is cheaper, you don't open with "we recommend an acquisition." You open with the unit economics that show the opportunity cost of 18 months of lost revenue, then let the numbers lead to the recommendation. The destination is the same. The path is completely different.

Most AI-generated strategy work is audience-blind. It structures arguments logically but not persuasively — because persuasion requires knowing what the reader already thinks and meeting them there.

## When to Apply

Activate this skill when the user is:
- Writing a board memo, executive recommendation, or strategy brief
- Preparing a pitch deck or investment committee presentation
- Drafting an internal proposal that needs stakeholder buy-in
- Creating any persuasive business communication where the audience matters
- Asking for help framing or positioning a recommendation

## Step 1: Identify the Audience

Before structuring any output, ask or infer:

**"Who is the primary reader, and what is their role in the decision?"**

Each audience type has a default lens — the filter through which they evaluate everything:

| Audience | Default Lens | What They Optimize For |
|---|---|---|
| Board of Directors — skeptical | Risk and downside | "What could go wrong, and are we protected?" |
| Board of Directors — aligned | Execution proof points | "Show me this will actually get done." |
| CFO / Finance Committee | Unit economics and returns | "What are the numbers, and when do we see payback?" |
| CEO / C-Suite (internal) | Organizational capacity | "Can we actually execute this, and who owns it?" |
| External Investors / IC | Thesis validation | "Does this fit our model, and what's the return profile?" |
| Senior Management Team | Ownership and timelines | "What do I need to do, by when, with what resources?" |

If the user hasn't specified the audience, ask directly. If they say "it's for the board," follow up: "Is this a board that's already supportive of this direction, or one that needs to be convinced?" The difference changes everything.

## Step 2: Surface What They Already Believe

This is the step most people skip — and it's the one that matters most.

Ask: **"What does this audience already believe about this decision? What are they anchored on?"**

Examples of audience anchors:
- "The CFO believes organic build is always cheaper than acquisition."
- "The board thinks our core market is stable and doesn't see urgency."
- "The investors are anchored on a 3x return threshold and skeptical of anything with a 5-year horizon."
- "The management team was burned by the last transformation initiative and is resistant to another one."

If the user doesn't know, help them infer based on the audience role:

> "A skeptical board is typically anchored on risk — they'll look for reasons to say no before they look for reasons to say yes. I'll structure the argument to address downside scenarios first, then build toward the opportunity. Does that match your read?"

The anchor determines your opening move. You must address it before you can advance your argument.

## Step 3: Choose the Leading Evidence

Different audiences are persuaded by different types of evidence. Lead with what resonates with your specific reader:

**Board — skeptical / risk-averse:**
- Open with: Risk mitigation and downside protection
- Lead evidence: Scenario analysis showing what happens if you do nothing, competitive threat timeline, cost of inaction
- Structure: Problem → risk of inaction → protected path forward → upside if it works
- Close with: Reversibility and off-ramps

**Board — already aligned:**
- Open with: Execution specificity
- Lead evidence: Implementation milestones, accountability matrix, 90-day quick wins that prove momentum
- Structure: Recommendation → proof of feasibility → execution plan → metrics that track progress
- Close with: First three decisions the board needs to make

**CFO / Finance Committee:**
- Open with: Unit economics and payback period
- Lead evidence: IRR, NPV, cash flow impact, margin trajectory, comparable transaction multiples
- Structure: Financial summary → investment required → return profile → sensitivity analysis → break-even timeline
- Close with: What the numbers look like if the key assumption is wrong by 20%

**CEO / C-Suite (internal):**
- Open with: Organizational capacity and sequencing
- Lead evidence: Resource requirements, capability gaps, dependencies between workstreams, talent implications
- Structure: Recommendation → what it requires from the org → sequencing and phasing → who owns what → escalation triggers
- Close with: The one thing that must be true for this to work

**External Investors / IC:**
- Open with: Thesis fit and return scenario
- Lead evidence: Market size, comparable valuations, entry multiple vs. expected exit, competitive moat durability
- Structure: Investment thesis → market validation → financial model → risk factors → return scenarios (base/bull/bear)
- Close with: Why now, and what changes if you wait 12 months

**Senior Management Team:**
- Open with: Clear ownership and resource allocation
- Lead evidence: Workstream breakdown, headcount/budget requirements, timeline with dependencies, decision rights
- Structure: What we're doing → why → who owns each piece → what resources shift → how we measure success → first 30 days
- Close with: What each leader in the room is responsible for

## Step 4: Adjust Tone and Emphasis

Beyond structure and evidence, calibrate these dimensions to the audience:

**Certainty level:** A skeptical audience needs hedged language with scenario ranges ("we estimate 15-22% margin improvement"). An aligned audience needs decisive language ("the target is 18% margin improvement by Q4").

**Detail depth:** A board needs the altitude — 3-5 key points with supporting logic. A management team needs the ground truth — specific owners, dates, budgets, dependencies.

**Jargon threshold:** Investors expect financial terminology (IRR, MOIC, TAM). Operators expect operational language (throughput, SLAs, headcount). Don't use finance language for an ops audience or vice versa.

**Urgency framing:** For a resistant audience, urgency comes from external threat ("the competitive window closes in Q3"). For an aligned audience, urgency comes from internal momentum ("we have 90 days to capture the advantage of moving first").

## Step 5: Validate the Frame

Before generating the full output, present the communication frame to the user:

> **Audience:** [role and disposition]
> **Their anchor:** [what they currently believe]
> **Opening move:** [what you lead with to meet them where they are]
> **Leading evidence:** [the type of proof that resonates]
> **Close:** [the ask or next step]
>
> "I'll structure the entire document around this framing. Does this match how you'd approach this audience?"

Adjust if the user corrects the audience read. The frame must be right before the content flows.
